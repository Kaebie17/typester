Difficulty sets passage length around what a strong typist (the average of the app target and your benchmark) finishes in the test time — roughly 430 / 500 / 600 words for Easy / Medium / Hard over 10 minutes. Unfinished words carry no penalty and aren’t counted wrong; they simply don’t add to your net speed. Daily set is the main source of fresh material. Once a day the app collects the top five to ten India-related headlines from official Indian sources (PIB, PRS and RBI, with a general news fallback) — headlines only, never article text — and sends all of them together in a single AI request. All of that happens on the server, so the page itself never contacts a news site and needs no proxy. Near-duplicate headlines are removed by keyword overlap before anything is sent. The model then writes two or three separate passages for each headline, one on the development itself, one on its background, one on the adjacent issues, so ten headlines become twenty to thirty distinct texts. Every passage is a completely new, self-contained piece of 420–480 words for each headline in neutral newspaper English, explaining the background and context rather than summarising anyone’s reporting, and proposes a descriptive title and a difficulty rating. The app then re-checks that rating with its own heuristic, which has the final say. The finished passages are cached on this device and are never regenerated unless the headlines change, so nothing is scraped, no article HTML is parsed, and no newspaper copy is ever reproduced. You can still paste passages of your own into My passages, and the exam-style set below works offline.
There is no error feedback while you type (as in the real exam) — the passage only underlines the current word in blue. After the test, open Review mistakes for a side-by-side comparison of the passage and what you typed, with wrong words marked. Scoring is word-by-word. TypingMaster mode (default) — the software JKSSB reportedly uses — counts every mismatched word as one full error (a wrong letter, a capital, or a spacing slip each spoil the whole word); Net WPM = Gross − (wrong words ÷ minutes). Full/Half mode is the SSC/UPSSSC convention where capitalization, punctuation, transposition and spacing count as ½ mistakes. The exact internal method JKSSB uses isn't officially published, so both are provided as close estimates — pick whichever your coaching material describes. Gross = (keystrokes ÷ 5) ÷ minutes; test is ~10 minutes; accuracy below 90% disqualifies; backspace only fixes the current word and carries no direct penalty. Marks = (net ÷ target top speed) × 20. If you enter the exam benchmark (the real highest net speed reported in the ongoing exam), the result also shows marks against that real figure — the closest estimate to your actual batch-relative score.
